# **Decoding brain tumors: an attention-guided hybrid learning framework for multi-class classification of MRI images** 

**Soham Bandyopadhyay[1] · Arnab Banerjee[2] · Bhumik Roy[2] · Ankur Lodh[2]**

## **Abstract** 

Brain tumors arising from glioma, meningioma, and pituitary regions present major clinical challenges because of their malignant potential and substantial impact on survival outcomes. Accurate and timely diagnosis remains essential to optimize therapeutic strategies, although most prior studies have concentrated on binary MRI-based classification with limited focus on systematic preprocessing. This work introduces an integrated framework for multi-class brain tumor prediction that incorporates sequential preprocessing steps including resizing, Gaussian blurring, and histogram equalization, followed by a hybrid CNN-Attention-SVM (CASVM) model. Convolutional layers are employed to extract hierarchical features, the attention mechanism selectively emphasizes salient tumor-related regions, and a One versus Rest SVM enables robust multi-class discrimination among glioma, meningioma, pituitary, and healthy cases. The method was evaluated on 7023 MRI scans obtained from Figshare, SARTAJ, and Br35H, achieving 96.86% ± 0.41% accuracy under fivefold crossvalidation, thereby surpassing conventional pipelines. These findings highlight the central role of systematic preprocessing and hybrid deep learning SVM integration in achieving accurate, generalizable, and computationally efficient brain tumor diagnostics.

## **2 Related work** 

Brain tumors are among the most critical neurological disorders, where timely and accurate identification is essential for effective intervention. Current research related to this area is briefly summarized below. 

Early CNN-based studies laid the foundation for automated brain tumor detection and classification from MRI scans. Dipu et al. [13] proposed a CNN framework that outperformed traditional feature-based methods on the BRATS dataset, demonstrating that deep learning captures complex spatial patterns often missed by manual features. Singh et al. [14] leveraged VGG16-based transfer learning to improve classification with limited annotated data. Based et al. [15] optimized CNN architectures for computational efficiency, showing that careful network design can reduce resource requirements while maintaining performance. Banerjee et al. [16] highlighted the impact of hyperparameter tuning on precision and robustness, and Singh et al. [17] achieved strong multi-class classification using T1-weighted contrast-enhanced MRI. 

Hybrid and transfer learning approaches further improved tumor detection by integrating pretrained models and ensemble strategies. Jain et al. [18] developed an ensemble deep learning framework (EDL-BTC) combining MobileNetV2, InceptionV3, and ResNet50, achieving superior accuracy. Liu et al. [19] used CNN-extracted features with SVM classifiers, achieving near-perfect performance in both binary and multi-class tumor tasks. These studies demonstrate that combining deep feature extraction with classical machine learning or ensemble strategies enhances both generalization and accuracy. 

Segmentation-focused methods enriched tumor delineation and localization. Akonda et al. [20] proposed a hybrid segmentation and classification pipeline for sharper boundaries and improved accuracy. Kavyasri et al. [21] applied federated learning, enabling collaborative model training across institutions while preserving patient privacy. Chaudhary et al. [22] combined clustering and wavelet-based segmentation with SVM, providing efficient tumor detection while balancing accuracy and computational cost. 

Parallel studies explored advanced architectures and computational paradigms. Gomaa et al. [23] applied anisotropic filtering with transfer learning for brain X-ray classification, demonstrating cross-modality potential. Combining different texture descriptors like LBP and WLD plays important role in medical image analysis field [24]. Dixit et al. [25] combined statistical feature extraction with Relevance Vector Machines for efficient classification. Sahoo et al. [26] integrated Residual U-Net with YOLOv2 for simultaneous segmentation and classification, achieving high accuracy with reduced manual effort. Ticku et al. [27] proposed a Quantum CNN using quantum embedding and qubit convolution, improving speed and efficiency in tumor detection.

## **4.3.1 Performance analysis of CASVM and other classifier** 

Here, CASVM, a hybrid deep learning–machine learning framework, combines CNN-based feature extraction, attention-driven refinement, and SVM classification to improve generalization, highlight salient spatial–channel patterns, handle limited data effectively, and provide robust decision boundaries (see Table 2). 

_CASVM-based classification on processed images_ The robustness of the proposed CASVM model in multi-class classification was confirmed by its consistently good performance across all evaluation measures (Table 3). It procures 96 _._ 86% _±_ 0 _._ 41% overall accuracy, 96 _._ 88% _±_ 0 _._ 41% for precision, 96 _._ 77% _±_ 0 _._ 36% for sensitivity, and 98 _._ 96% _±_ 0 _._ 41% for specificity. At 99 _._ 34% _±_ 0 _._ 13%, the AUC was noticeably high, demonstrating exceptional class separability. Stable and consistent performance across 5-fold cross-validation is highlighted by minimal standard deviations, emphasizing the CASVM framework’s generalizability. For every fold, the confusion matrices are displayed in Fig. 4. 

_CNN and OvR-SVM based approach_ The CASVM framework, integrating CNN-based feature extraction with a OvR-SVM classifier, was benchmarked against its individual components. Baseline experiments using standalone CNN or SVM achieved strong performance; however, CASVM consistently outperformed both across all metrics 

Understanding the influence of various components in brain tumor prediction, an ablation study was conducted as follows: 

_(1) Impact of excluding the attention mechanism:_ Here, the CNN-derived feature vector is directly input to the OvRSVM (Table 4). The CNN-attention with SVM achieved an accuracy of 95.13% ± 0.90%, precision 95.03% ± 0.91%, sensitivity 94.94% ± 0.93%, specificity 95.03% ± 0.91%, and AUC 99.29% ± 0.34%. Results show that attention improves feature refinement and model robustness. 

_(2) Replacing OvR-SVM with XGBoost:_ The attentionoptimized CNN features were classified using XGBoost (Table 4), yielding accuracy 94.80% ± 0.91%, precision 94.65% ± 0.94%, sensitivity 94.62% ± 0.88%, specificity 94.65% ± 0.94%, and AUC 99.18% ± 0.01%. While XGBoost generalizes well, SVM provides superior multiclass discrimination. 

_(3) Effect of varying CNN filters:_ Excluding attention, the number of convolutional filters was varied (16, 32, 64) (Table 4). The model achieved accuracy 95.24% ± 0.96%, precision 95.12% ± 0.98%, sensitivity 95.05% ± 0.99%, specificity 95.12% ± 0.98%, and AUC 99.26% ± 0.26%. Results confirm robust performance, though the proposed method remains superior. 

_(4) Effect of varying CNN layers:_ Using four convolutional layers (16, 32, 64, 128) without attention (Table 4) resulted in accuracy 95.19% ± 0.78%, precision 95.07% ± 0.84%, sensitivity 95.02% ± 0.80%, specificity 95.07% ± 0.84%, and AUC 99.37% ± 0.14%. While effective, the proposed CASVM consistently achieves higher performance, highlighting its ability to capture complex discriminative features.
